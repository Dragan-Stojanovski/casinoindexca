How wagering requirements actually work

A wagering requirement (also called playthrough or a rollover) is the total amount you must bet before bonus winnings become withdrawable cash. It's expressed as a multiplier — 35×, for example — and the number it multiplies matters enormously. A 35× requirement on a "bonus only" basis with a $200 bonus means $7,000 of bets. The same 35× on a "deposit plus bonus" basis, after a $200 deposit and $200 bonus, means $14,000 — double the work for the identical headline. The calculator above makes this concrete, and the "applies to" toggle is the single most important switch in it.

The reason wagering costs you money isn't just time — it's the house edge grinding away on every one of those forced bets. Each dollar you're required to wager carries the game's built-in edge, so the more you must bet, the more you can expect to lose along the way. That's why "real expected value" can turn negative on a big bonus: the cost of clearing it exceeds the bonus itself. A no-wagering reward skips this entirely, which is why even a small one can beat a large sticky bonus.

The hidden terms that gut a "good" bonus

Wagering is the headline trap, but several quieter clauses can quietly destroy a bonus's value. Learn to check all of them before you claim.

  • Game weighting. Slots usually count 100% toward wagering, but blackjack and other high-RTP games often count 10% or even 0%. A "great for table players" bonus that excludes tables is worthless to a blackjack player.
  • Maximum bet while wagering. Many bonuses cap your bet (often $5) until the wagering is cleared. Breach it, even by accident, and you can forfeit the whole bonus and its winnings.
  • Maximum cashout / win cap. A "no wagering" free-spin reward can still cap winnings at, say, $50 — so a lucky $500 spin only pays $50. Always read the cap.
  • Time limits. A short expiry (7 days is common) can force rushed, larger bets that increase your losses. No-wagering rewards rarely suffer this.
  • Sticky vs non-sticky. A "sticky" bonus can never be withdrawn — only winnings above it — while the bonus itself vanishes on cashout. Cash rewards and cashback avoid this entirely.

What is a no wagering casino bonus?

A no-wagering bonus lets you withdraw any winnings immediately, with no playthrough requirement. Winnings are paid as real cash rather than restricted bonus funds. True no-wagering offers are rare; low-wagering offers (5x–10x) are more common and nearly as good.

Why is low wagering better?

A big bonus with 40x wagering can be almost impossible to clear, while a smaller no- or low-wagering reward is money you can actually keep. Always compare the wagering, not just the headline amount.

How do I calculate wagering on a bonus?

Multiply the amount by the wagering number. A $50 bonus at 40× needs $2,000 in bets to clear; at 5× it's just $250. Our bonus calculator does the maths for any offer instantly.

Is cashback better than a deposit bonus?

Often, yes — cashback is usually paid with very low or no wagering (as low as 5×), so it behaves like real money. A big deposit match at 40× can be far harder to convert into a withdrawal.

Do free spins count as no wagering?

Rarely. Most free-spin winnings carry both wagering and a win cap. True "no wagering" spins pay winnings as cash — always read whether the reward is cash or sticky bonus funds.

What is game weighting on a casino bonus?

Game weighting is how much each game contributes to clearing wagering. Slots usually count 100%, but blackjack and other high-RTP games often count only 10% or 0%. A bonus that excludes tables is worthless for a table-game player, so always check the weighting before claiming.

Is it better to play with no bonus at all?

For many players, yes. Playing with your own deposit keeps every dollar withdrawable at any time, with no rollover, bet cap or expiry. It's the best choice when your priority is fast, unrestricted access to your money.

What does a sticky bonus mean?

A sticky bonus can never be withdrawn — only winnings above the bonus amount can, and the bonus itself is removed when you cash out. Cash rewards and cashback are non-sticky, behaving like real money, which is why they're usually the better value.
