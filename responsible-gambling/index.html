Across Canada

Every province offers a problem-gambling helpline and self-exclusion program. Your provincial health line can connect you to local support, and the Canada Safety Council publishes guidance on gambling risk.

Signs it may be becoming a problem

  • Spending more time or money than you planned, or chasing losses.
  • Borrowing money, or gambling with money meant for essentials.
  • Feeling anxious, secretive or irritable about your gambling.
  • Gambling to escape stress rather than for entertainment.

If any of these feel familiar, reaching out early makes a real difference. Support is judgment-free and confidential.

Tools that help you stay in control

  • Deposit and time limits — set them in your account before you play, not after.
  • Reality checks — session reminders that show how long you've been playing.
  • Cool-off periods — short breaks that lock your account for a set time.
  • Self-exclusion — longer-term exclusion from an operator or, in Ontario, across regulated sites.

We do not index or recommend operators that make responsible-gambling tools hard to find or hard to use. Easy limits and clear self-exclusion count toward an operator's Safety Index.

Playing responsibly

  • Treat gambling as paid entertainment, not a way to make money.
  • Only ever play with money you can comfortably afford to lose.
  • Set a budget and a time limit before you start — and keep to them.
  • Never gamble to recover losses, and take regular breaks.
  • You must be 19+ (or the legal age in your province) to play.

If you're worried about yourself or someone else, contact a support service above. You don't have to wait until things feel out of control to ask for help.

A quick self-check

If you're unsure whether your gambling is still healthy, a few honest questions can help. Have you ever felt you needed to bet more money to get the same excitement? Have you tried to cut back and struggled? Do you gamble to escape worry or low mood, or to win back losses? Have you lied to anyone about how much you gamble, or gambled money meant for bills? Answering "yes" to even one of these is a signal worth taking seriously — and a good reason to talk to one of the free, confidential services above. These questions echo widely used screening tools, but they are not a diagnosis; a support worker can help you understand where you stand.

Setting a budget that actually works

The single most effective habit is deciding what gambling costs you before you start, and treating it like any other entertainment expense. Set a fixed amount you're comfortable losing entirely, and stop when it's gone rather than topping up to chase it back. Use the deposit and loss limits every casino we list provides — setting them in advance is far easier than showing restraint mid-session. Keep gambling money separate from money for essentials, never borrow to play, and bank a withdrawal when you're ahead rather than rolling it back in. Prepaid methods like Paysafecard can act as a hard cap, since you can only ever play what's on the voucher.

Myths worth unlearning

  • "I'm due for a win." Every spin and hand is independent. Past results never change future odds — there is no such thing as being "due".
  • "I can win it back." Chasing losses is the fastest route to bigger losses. The house edge doesn't pause because you're behind.
  • "A system beats the house." No betting system overcomes the built-in edge over time; strategies like Martingale simply risk larger losses.
  • "It's an investment." Gambling is entertainment with a cost, not a way to make money. Treating it as income is where trouble starts.

Worried about someone else?

Gambling harm affects families and friends, not just the person gambling. If you're concerned about someone, you don't need their permission to seek advice — the helplines above support affected others too. Approach the conversation without judgment, at a calm moment, focusing on what you've noticed and how you feel rather than accusations. Avoid taking over their finances impulsively or lending money to cover losses, which can prolong the harm. Encourage them toward a support service, and look after your own wellbeing as well.

Finding support across Canada

Every province and territory funds free, confidential problem-gambling support and a self-exclusion program. In Ontario, ConnexOntario (1-866-531-2600) offers 24/7 help and connects players to services, and the province operates self-exclusion across regulated iGaming Ontario sites. Elsewhere, your provincial health line or gambling-support service — such as those run by provincial health authorities and lottery/gaming corporations — can connect you to local counselling and exclusion tools. If you're ever in crisis, please contact 9-8-8 (Suicide Crisis Helpline) or your local emergency number.
