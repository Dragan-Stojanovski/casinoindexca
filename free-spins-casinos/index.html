The clauses that decide a spins offer's value

Three terms matter most. The value per spin sets the real play value — 100 spins at $0.10 is $10 of play, not a fortune. The win cap is the big one: a "no wagering" or generous spins reward can still cap winnings at, say, $50, so a lucky $500 spin only pays $50. And the wagering requirement on any winnings determines how much you must bet before cashing out. A large spin count with a low per-spin value, a tight win cap and heavy wagering can be worth less than a smaller, fairer offer. Use the estimator to see how a win cap changes everything.

Do free spins winnings have wagering requirements?

Usually yes. Winnings from free spins are typically credited as bonus funds you must wager a set number of times before withdrawing, and often carry a maximum-win cap. True no-wagering spins, which pay winnings as cash, are rarer but the most valuable.

What is a free spins win cap?

A win cap limits how much you can keep from free-spin winnings — for example, a $50 cap means even a lucky $500 spin only pays $50. Always check the cap; it's the clause that most often reduces the real value of a big-sounding spins offer.

How much is a free spin worth?

Each free spin has a fixed stake value set by the casino, often $0.10–$0.20. So 100 spins at $0.20 is $20 of play. The realistic expected return is the total spin value times the game's RTP, minus any wagering cost — usually a modest amount, so treat spins as fun rather than a guaranteed win.
