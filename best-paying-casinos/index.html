What is RTP, and why does it matter?

RTP (Return to Player) is the percentage of all money wagered that a game pays back over millions of spins. A slot with a 96% RTP returns, on average, $96 for every $100 staked — the other 4% is the house edge. RTP doesn't predict any single session (short-term results swing wildly), but over time a higher-RTP game simply keeps more money in your pocket.

RTP vs volatility — know the difference

RTP is how much a game returns; volatility is how it returns it. A high-volatility slot can share the same 96% RTP as a low-volatility one but pays in rarer, bigger hits. If you want longer sessions, favour lower volatility; if you're chasing a big win, higher volatility suits — just size your bets accordingly.

How to check a game's RTP in 30 seconds

You never have to guess — reputable studios publish it. Here's the routine:

  1. Open the game and tap the menu, "i" or paytable icon.
  2. Find "RTP" or "Return to Player" — it's usually near the rules or game info.
  3. Compare versions. Some studios ship the same slot at different RTPs (e.g., 96.5% vs 94%); casinos choose which to run, so it pays to check.

If a game hides its RTP entirely, that's a small red flag — the best-paying casinos stock studios that are transparent about it.

RTP myths, debunked

  • "A 96% RTP means I'll get $96 back." No — it's a long-run average over millions of spins, not a per-session guarantee. Short sessions swing wildly.
  • "A game is 'due' to pay." Every spin is independent. Past results don't change future odds.
  • "Higher RTP means frequent wins." Not necessarily — that's volatility, a separate measure. A 96% high-volatility slot pays rarely but bigger.
  • "The casino sets the RTP." The game provider sets it; the casino only chooses which version to offer.

High RTP vs jackpots — the trade-off

Progressive jackpot slots (like Mega Moolah) often carry a lower base RTP because a slice of every bet feeds the jackpot pool. That's a deliberate trade: worse day-to-day odds in exchange for a shot at a life-changing prize. If steady value is your aim, favour high-RTP non-jackpot slots and low-house-edge tables. The highest-RTP games in detail

Blackjack — the mathematician's game

Played with correct basic strategy, blackjack has one of the lowest house edges anywhere on the casino floor — often around 0.5%, giving an effective RTP near 99.5%. The catch is that this figure assumes you make the mathematically correct decision on every hand, using a printed or memorised strategy chart. Deviate — take insurance, stand when you should hit — and the edge climbs. Rule variations matter too: blackjack paying 3:2 is far better than 6:5, and fewer decks generally favour the player. For pure long-run value, a live blackjack table with good rules is the single best bet in the building.

Video poker — skill meets transparency

Full-pay video poker games such as 9/6 Jacks or Better can return over 99% with optimal play, and unlike slots the paytable is right there for you to read before you commit a cent. Because the returns depend on your decisions, video poker rewards study — but even casual correct play keeps you close to break-even. It's the most under-rated high-RTP option in most Canadian casino lobbies.

Baccarat and roulette — low-effort value

Baccarat's banker bet carries a house edge of just over 1%, and it requires no strategy beyond consistently backing the banker. European (single-zero) roulette sits around 2.7% — dramatically better than the American double-zero wheel at 5.26%. If a casino offers both, always choose the single-zero table; it literally halves the house's edge.

Slots — where RTP varies most

Slots are where players lose the most value simply by not checking. Two games that look almost identical can run at 96.5% and 94%, a gap that compounds heavily over a long session — as our calculator above demonstrates. Some studios even ship a single title at multiple RTP settings and let the casino pick. The best-paying casinos tend to run the higher configurations and stock makers who publish the exact number in the game's info panel.

RTP, volatility and your bankroll — putting it together

RTP tells you the long-run cost of playing; volatility tells you how bumpy the ride will be along the way. The two interact in ways that matter for how you set your stake. A high-RTP, low-volatility game (think many video slots or blackjack) grinds close to even with frequent small results — ideal if you want a long, steady session on a modest budget. A high-RTP, high-volatility game returns the same percentage over time but delivers it in rare, large hits, which means you need a deeper bankroll and a smaller bet size to survive the dry spells.

A practical rule: the higher the volatility, the smaller your bet relative to your total bankroll should be. Betting 1–2% of your bankroll per spin on a volatile slot gives you enough rounds to reach the big features that make the RTP real. Bet 10% a spin and variance will very likely bust you before the math has a chance to play out — even on a generous 96.5% game.

What does RTP mean at an online casino?

RTP (Return to Player) is the percentage of all wagers a game pays back over the long run. A 96% RTP slot returns $96 for every $100 wagered on average; the remaining 4% is the house edge.

Which casino games have the highest RTP?

Blackjack (up to ~99.5% with basic strategy), video poker like Jacks or Better, and certain low-house-edge table games pay the most. Among slots, RTPs typically range from 94% to 97%+.

Do bigger casinos pay better?

Not automatically — but casinos with large libraries from top studios give you access to more high-RTP titles. RTP is set by the game provider, so library quality and breadth matter.

Where can I see a slot's RTP?

Open the game and check the menu, "i" or paytable — reputable studios list the RTP there. Some ship the same slot at different RTPs, so it's worth confirming which version a casino runs.

Is a high RTP the same as a high win rate?

No. RTP is how much a game returns over the long run; volatility is how often and how big it pays. A high-RTP, high-volatility slot pays rarely but large; a low-volatility one pays smaller and more often.

Why do jackpot slots have lower RTP?

A portion of every bet feeds the progressive jackpot pool, which lowers the base RTP. You're trading day-to-day odds for a shot at a life-changing prize.

How much does a higher RTP actually save me?

It compounds with volume. Wagering $500 on a 96.7% game costs about $16.50 in theory, versus $30 on a 94% game — nearly double the expected loss for the same play. Over many sessions the gap becomes very large, which is why choosing high-RTP games matters. Try the calculator above with your own numbers.

Does the casino control a game's RTP?

The game provider sets the RTP, but some studios ship a title at multiple RTP configurations and let the casino choose which to run. The best-paying casinos tend to run the higher settings and stock studios that publish the exact figure in the game info panel.

Which bet on roulette has the best odds?

European (single-zero) roulette has a house edge of about 2.7%, versus 5.26% on American double-zero wheels. Always choose the single-zero table when available — it roughly halves the house edge regardless of which bets you place.
